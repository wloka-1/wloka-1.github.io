AI Platform Design

Designing how a 240-year-old bank manages AI

BNY · Senior UX Designer · via Blink · Oct 2025 to Present

BNY is building Eliza, an internal AI platform built on Claude where "digital employees" are recruited, monitored, and governed like human talent. I lead design across navigation, the marketplace, dashboards, personalization, and the design system that has to feed AI agents instead of human designers.

The platform

Eliza sits under BNY's AI umbrella. Underneath the umbrella: chat (the AI assistant), a Digital Employee marketplace (where AI agents live), an initiative intake system (how new AI projects get started), and governance tools. When I joined, these areas had grown fast but the UX hadn't kept up. Multiple product areas were siloed under inconsistent navigation. Users couldn't find what they needed. Leadership couldn't see across the platform.

My job was to make it cohesive while shipping on aggressive timelines through a complex stakeholder landscape.

Navigation redesign

The existing Eliza platform used a workbench-style top navigation that couldn't scale. As BNY added more AI products (governance tools, performance dashboards, compliance workflows), the top nav was running out of room and users were getting lost.

I proposed a hierarchical IA with four top-level areas (Eliza, Governance, Performance, Compliance) unified under a left-nav with a hub landing page at AI.bny. Significant structural change. The team needed something transformational enough to solve real problems and shippable enough to launch in March.

Key decisions

  • Left nav over top nav. Scales as product areas expand. Hides when users drill into specific tools to maximize workspace.
  • Hub landing page. AI.bny becomes the front door. Eliza (chat) is the first item in navigation, not the entire platform. Reframes Eliza as one part of BNY's AI ecosystem.
  • Logo changed from "Eliza" to "BNY". Subtle but important. The platform is bigger than the chatbot.
  • Chat history in the nav. Pulls from local backend so users have persistent access to prior conversations.

I ran independent user research alongside the design work. Interviewed Eliza users 1-on-1 about what they needed. Fed transcripts through an agent I built to synthesize findings. Designed off the synthesis. No research process was handed to me. I built the loop.

We launched the navigation redesign, marketplace redesign, chat history, and new landing page together. A coordinated release that changed how BNY's employees experience AI at the company.

Digital Employee Marketplace

BNY's digital employees are AI agents that handle specific tasks (transaction repair, document processing, compliance checks). Each has capabilities, performance metrics, and a lifecycle (pipeline → pilot → active → retired). The marketplace is where these agents live.

I owned the UX end-to-end. Defined the data model (Role → Capability → Process → Agent instance → Tasks), the filtering and integration surfaces across Practices/Platforms/Hubs, and three deployment pathways (net-new, clone-and-modify, scale existing). These patterns now compound across every downstream agent-creation flow.

I also designed the manager dashboard with cross-platform capability views, so a manager could see all the DEs they're responsible for across different systems. Health status uses a red/amber/green system based on accuracy thresholds. If an agent drops below 94% accuracy, it triggers a warning.

Design challenges

  • Table consistency. Different DE types have different data. Transaction repair agents have transaction volumes. Engineering DEs don't. Needed a table structure that worked across all agent types without blank columns everywhere.
  • Naming. "Username" was confusing. Landed on "DE ID" for the system identifier and "Employee name" for the human-readable name.
  • Disable/enable flow. Managers need to take agents offline without losing their configuration. Modal confirmation for disable, re-enable without starting over.

Light mode in three days

The team had 1,100+ hard-coded color references across 138 files in the monorepo, with dark/light tokens partially built but no working switching mechanism. Direct color inversion didn't achieve the desired aesthetic. The product had launched in light mode anyway, with quality that nobody was happy with.

I built a new V3 in light mode with a Claude → code workflow. Token-only consumption from the new design system, A11Y-compliant palette explorations (slate, frost, pewter, steel), direct foundation-layer token mirroring for dark to light conversion. Working prototype in three days.

Alongside it I built an automated design audit tool that screenshots inconsistencies across the system and files them as issue-tracker entries. The team gets faster QA without doing the QA. That tool keeps paying back.

Shaping the AI-consumable design system

Before paternity leave I shaped the strategy for an AI-consumable design system that becomes the foundation every product area inherits. The concrete moves:

  • Bent in-flight teams off legacy approaches. Drove the decision to rip Tailwind from a key product because the new system wouldn't survive it.
  • Pushed component strategy toward LLM-friendly architecture. Larger semantic components with clear usage contracts instead of highly compositional Swiss Army knife patterns. Larger component APIs require more LLM processing and validation cycles. Smaller, focused, semantically-named components are easier for both humans and agents.
  • Surfaced where traditional design systems break down for AI-driven UI. When AI is generating the UI, the system isn't a component library. It's instructions.

A colleague carried the specific multi-layer architecture and component-contract implementation across the finish line during my paternity leave. The strategic shape was set beforehand.

How I work, applied

The biggest single impact I've had at BNY might not be any specific screen. It's how design feedback happens.

My workflow is Claude → code. I no longer touch Figma. Time goes to collecting context, then iterating, testing, and shipping. On one occasion I delivered five prototype iterations in a single session: record a walkthrough with stakeholders, process notes in 10 minutes, iterate in 2 hours total. Compare that to the previous cycle of Figma mockup → review meeting → comments → revision → another meeting → handoff spec. Weeks compressed to hours.

The team is several months ahead of peer design groups on AI tool adoption. I also pushed for recording all product team meetings and advocated for user-testing data backing design decisions in leadership demos, rather than just showing mockups and hoping for buy-in.

What this shows

The hardest problem wasn't the UI. It was defining what "managing AI" means for a bank that's older than the country. I shaped the conceptual framework that the team designs within: agent lifecycle, governance integration, observability, platform IA. I also built force-multiplier tooling alongside the design work, because the highest-leverage thing a staff designer can do is make the rest of the team faster.

Defining the taxonomy

Before you can build a design system, you need a shared language for what things are. I defined the taxonomy that became QDS's organizing principle: Atoms → Molecules → Organisms → Templates → UX Flows → Design Patterns.

This wasn't an atomic-design rehash. The addition of UX Flows and Design Patterns as explicit levels acknowledged that Qualcomm's products needed more than component specs. They needed guidance on how components compose into real user experiences. A button is an atom. A search bar is a molecule. "How filtering works across all Qualcomm products" is a pattern and deserved its own place in the hierarchy.

This taxonomy informed everything downstream: how the documentation was structured, how engineering understood the relationships between parts of the system, and how the team talked about what they were building.

The AI documentation pipeline

The math problem: 60+ components that needed full documentation. Manually, that's maybe 12 components in the time we had. The system would launch half-empty.

I built an AI-in-the-loop documentation pipeline using Claude. The workflow: I created a structured input format that captured a component's props, variants, states, and design intent. Fed that into Claude with carefully engineered prompts. Claude generated a first draft. Human designers reviewed, refined, approved. CMS pages were created from the output.

What made it work

  • Structured inputs, not freeform. The quality of AI output depends entirely on input quality. I designed a template that forced the right information to be captured before generation started.
  • Human-in-the-middle, not human-out-of-the-loop. Every doc was reviewed and refined by a designer. The AI did the heavy lifting. Humans ensured accuracy and voice consistency.
  • The process was the product. I didn't just build the pipeline. I defined the input format, wrote the prompts, established the review process, and trained the team on the workflow. When I left, the team could keep going without me.

Result: 120x reduction in writing effort, 95% reduction in CMS page creation time, 60+ components documented on schedule. The system launched complete instead of half-built.

1,500+ icons, end-to-end

Qualcomm had icons scattered across teams with no standards, no naming conventions, and no process for adding new ones. Marketing used one set. Product used another.

I owned the icon library from audit to ship. Cataloged every existing icon across both marketing and product design. Established sizing standards and naming conventions. Built a metadata schema so icons could be searchable and filterable. Created a governance workflow for custom icon submissions. Shipped v1 with 1,500+ utility and marketing icons unified across all three brands.

Documentation architecture

A design system is only as useful as its documentation site. I designed the complete information architecture: sitemap, content structure, navigation, and page templates. Evaluated CMS options (including Knapsack) before the team decided to build a custom storefront, which I collaborated with engineering to implement.

Each component page followed a consistent template: overview, usage guidelines, variants, states, accessibility, code reference. This consistency meant that once you learned how to read one component page, you could read them all.

Defining what a pattern is, then which ones the system would ship

QDS didn't have an answer to the question "what counts as a UX pattern, and which ones belong in this system." Without that answer, every team picks their own definition and the system fragments before it ships. I owned the work to define both.

Original research. Interviewed three staff designers and a Head of Design to surface what they were already calling patterns, where they disagreed, and which behaviors caused the most rework. Benchmarked peer documentation systems (Carbon, Material, Polaris, Atlassian) to understand how the industry was framing the problem. Synthesized into a working definition and a candidate taxonomy.

Tested the taxonomy across current QC products and the shape of what was coming next. Navigation was the highest-leverage cluster — Top Nav, Side Nav, Browser Nav, Local Nav, Marketing Nav each had different rules, different states, different audiences. I deconstructed real product surfaces and mapped them against the proposed taxonomy to validate it survived contact with the actual portfolio.

Patterns aren't just happy paths. Each pattern got state coverage: forms documented by layout and validation state, tables documented across empty / loading / unauthorized / vague-input states. State coverage is what makes a pattern usable for product teams who would otherwise reinvent the same broken edges.

Then I presented to the Heads of Design to get buy-in on the definition and the taxonomy. Patterns work doesn't matter until leadership agrees that's how the org is going to talk about patterns going forward. That alignment is what made the pattern library roadmap real instead of a side artifact.

Governance the system needed to survive a distributed team

A system without governance is a library that slowly rots. I established review workflows, contribution processes, and quality standards that let distributed teams across multiple time zones and business units contribute without the system losing coherence.

What this shows

Two pillars of staff-level systems work. First: AI as a first-class tool in DS operations (defined the input format, wrote the prompts, set up the review loop, trained the team) so the system could ship complete instead of half-built. Second: original research and taxonomy work that defined the abstraction layer the whole team designed inside of — and the stakeholder management to get Heads of Design to agree on it before it shipped.

I was also the lead designer for Navigation, building a framework used by 40M+ monthly active users, and contributed scalable components to the design system serving 40+ designers.

Context

I got dropped into the middle of this project, so typical discovery and exploration was out. As the only designer on a team of three leading this initiative, partnered with a PM who had not yet fully come to appreciate the benefits of design thinking, advocating for design thinking without delaying the project was a tool I quickly deployed and sharpened.

I aligned on four business and user objectives:

  • Reduce cost. Decrease call volume by providing accurate results through auto-suggested terms and categorized results that fit users' mental model.
  • Improve usability. Reduce friction by guiding users to intended results through auto-suggestions, relevant filters, and improved content taxonomies.
  • Increase revenue. Guide users from search to Shop, reducing drop-off.
  • Improve CSAT. Reduce dead ends by providing auto-suggestions and relevant results from initial search through filtered results.

Research & analysis

To maintain project deadlines while balancing user needs, I led research initiatives end-to-end: defined the research questions, briefed the in-house research team and third-party vendors on methodology, and translated findings into product direction.

  • Conducted interviews with the research team to gain deeper insights into known search patterns across several user journeys
  • Analyzed user behavior through Clicktale heatmaps to understand current search pain points and interaction patterns
  • Referenced Baymard Institute and Nielsen Norman Group for established search UX best practices
  • Synthesized quantitative data from user tracking to understand common patterns and validate assumptions

Precedent analysis

I conducted competitive and comparative analysis across Verizon, Google, REI, Amazon, Home Depot, and Dell, drawing insights from industry leaders to balance innovation with established patterns.

  • Verizon. Tabular filtering and near-matches below top hits.
  • Google. Type-ahead with contextual suggestions.
  • REI. Type-ahead that surfaces genuinely helpful content.
  • Amazon. Highlights near-perfect matches with visual distinction. Multi-link "near match" results.
  • Home Depot. Type-ahead loads potentially helpful content for ambiguous queries.
  • Dell. Separates shopping and support into tabs, reducing cognitive load.

Design & iteration

I developed design concepts addressing user needs within technical constraints, iterating rapidly based on stakeholder feedback. Five major iterations:

  • V1.02. Basic full-screen takeover search.
  • V1.03. Introduced auto-suggestions.
  • V1.04. Content categorization (phones, support, accessories) with chip-style filters.
  • V1.05. Refined visual hierarchy with tabbed categories and inline product results.
  • V1.06. Contextual results pages with smart filtering.

Key design decisions

  • Zero-state design. Popular categories and recent searches, reducing the blank-page problem.
  • Auto-suggestions with categories. Results grouped by type so users immediately understand what they're looking at.
  • Chip-based filters. Lightweight, mobile-friendly filtering without a separate panel.
  • "Looking for Sprint results?" Contextual messaging for legacy Sprint users after the T-Mobile merger.

Testing & validation

Implemented A/B testing to validate design decisions before full deployment. Continuously monitored key metrics through Coveo's administration console. The numbers: 94% null result reduction, 105% engagement increase, 661% buying intention increase.

The final design

Reflection

Within a short period and with a high-visibility feature at stake:

  • I advocated for design thinking and users when others were focused on the business
  • I kept a level head in a high-pressure situation
  • I delivered impact in a small team while navigating a complex organization, within time constraints

What this shows

The metrics are strong, but the real story is what happened around them. I was the only designer, dropped mid-project, working with a PM who didn't yet value design thinking. I advocated for users when others were focused on the business. I delivered impact in a compressed timeline in a complex organization. That's the same muscle senior product design requires across any ecosystem: influence without authority, data-informed craft, and the judgment to know when good enough is done.

Discovery & research

I leaned on qualitative user research, precedent analysis, collaborative team decisions, and my own instincts when making design decisions.

Over three years I started multiple "Core User" groups, soliciting direct feedback from community members. This wasn't formal UXR. It was embedded, ongoing, community-native research. I was in Telegram chats daily, talking to users, watching how they interacted with the product, and using that signal to inform design decisions.

  • Telegram groups. Invite-only OG groups for direct feedback and ideation. Established rapport with power users who became our sharpest critics.
  • Discord channels. Announced features, gauged reactions, used emoji polls for quick signal on new UI directions.
  • Twitter surveys. Ran polls on market preferences and feature priorities. One poll (67 votes) directly influenced our market expansion roadmap.

Design strategy

Early on I developed a Design Strategy document, a "Design Ethos" that balanced Overlay's highly technical nature with a playful, meme-driven brand identity.

The core question: how do you design a protocol that's supposed to handle everything? In creation, if you design for everything, nothing has significance. The spectrum of possibilities is too great.

The answer: Cats.

Our team had a deep appreciation of scientific rigor, mathematical dexterity, and the absurd. Few things are as beautifully absurd as cats and our social infatuation with them. They're obsessive, they're the second most successful hunters in the animal kingdom, they're cool, fuzzy, and turned out to be an aesthetic we liked.

What does a cat do if it doesn't like something on the shelf? It knocks it off. This philosophy ("scientists and cats") became the foundation for all marketing, communication, and product personality.

Design principles from the ethos

  • Shapes. Circles from the logo: absurdist, organic, subjective. Squares: math, science, equal, objective.
  • Patterns. Western progression: left to right, top to bottom. Type left-aligned, buttons bottom-right. Simple shapes define simple patterns.
  • "Packet" pattern. A function like "build" or "unwind" lives as a packet: housed within a square with 1px border and 4px radius.

Collaboration & systems

I collaborated closely with dev on all new features. When the team was small, I cultivated a relationship with our front-end lead that allowed us to move extremely fast without formal documentation.

I built a component library on the fly: buttons, tabs, spacing, dropdowns, headers, market elements, cards, pop-ups, radio buttons. Notes for devs were embedded directly in the library rather than separate spec documents.

Iterative design

Four major app versions, each responding to real market feedback:

  • V1. Fit DeFi summer trends. Simple, functional, dark. Built for always-online on-chain traders.
  • V2. Increased legibility but landing on the product still caused confusion.
  • V3. Landed on a list of products. Quick scan of tradable markets with top navigation functionality.
  • V4. Most refined. Polished visual language for broader user base. Featured markets, governance, chess grandmasters index.

Brand evolution

I engaged a brand design agency to move Overlay from the initial phase to a brand with greater gravitas. We started simple and mathematical. We progressed into nuanced and fluid. The pixel art cat became a samurai cat.

Referral program

What I learned

  • Speed to market is almost more important than anything else when you're a young company.
  • Cultivating dev relationships creates +1 effects on design velocity.
  • How-to, and how-not-to, build a startup.

What this shows

Founding a company teaches you to design with extreme constraints, make irreversible decisions with incomplete information, and build for a community, not just users. The "scientists and cats" philosophy demonstrated that technical products can have personality. The developer collaboration muscle is directly transferable to any product team that wants to ship faster.
